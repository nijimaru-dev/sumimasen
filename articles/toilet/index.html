Japanese Toilets: Every Button Explained

Culture · 4 min read · By Nijimaru · June 2026
This article contains no affiliate links. About our disclosures →

The control panel on a Japanese washlet toilet looks like it belongs on a commercial aircraft. Most visitors either stare at it, press nothing, or press the wrong thing with predictable results. Here's the full translation.

The buttons (in order of importance)

🚽 Flush (大 / 小)

大 (dai) = large flush. 小 (sho) = small flush. These are almost always on the wall panel or on a separate handle. Use 大 for a full flush, 小 for liquid waste. Some toilets flush automatically when you stand up.

🔊 Sound (音姫 / 擬音)

The "sound princess" button. Plays a flushing sound to mask bathroom noise. Press once to activate, press the stop button when done. This is not the flush — a very common mistake.

Do not confuse this with the flush. The sound button icon usually looks like a speaker or musical note. Pressing it by accident is harmless; pressing it instead of the flush is not.

🚿 Bidet (おしり / ビデ)

おしり (oshiri) = rear wash. ビデ (bide) = front wash (female hygiene). There's usually a pressure slider and sometimes a position adjustment. Start with low pressure if you're trying it for the first time. You've been warned.

💨 Dryer (乾燥)

Warm air dryer. Works, but slow. Most people still use toilet paper. Located on the panel and activated after the bidet cycle.

🌡️ Seat warmer (暖房便座)

The heated seat is usually always on (set by the establishment). You'll encounter this in winter and initially assume something is wrong. Nothing is wrong. It's warm because it's supposed to be warm.

🚪 Lid open/close (ふた開 / ふた閉)

Some high-end toilets open the lid automatically when you enter the stall. If you see open/close buttons, these are for the lid. Not the flush.

Finding the flush

If you can't find the flush button, look in this order:

  1. The wall panel (most common — the large button labeled 大)
  2. The side of the toilet tank (lever or button)
  3. The toilet seat panel (smaller dedicated button)
  4. A motion sensor above the toilet (wave your hand)

Squat toilets

Still exist in older buildings, some train stations, and rural areas. Face the hood (the raised porcelain end), squat down, and flush the handle at the back. Western-style stalls are usually available in the same bathroom — look for the 洋式 (yoshiki) sign.

Finding toilets

Japan has excellent public bathroom infrastructure. If you need one:

  • Any convenience store (free, clean, open 24 hours)
  • Train stations — usually near the ticket gates
  • Department store basement floors
  • Parks and temples (variable quality)

Tipping is not expected and there are no pay toilets in standard use. Just walk in.

Key buttons

  • 大 / 小 = flush (large/small)
  • 音姫 = sound (not flush!)
  • おしり = rear wash
  • 乾燥 = dryer
