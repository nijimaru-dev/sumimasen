Japan Manners: The 9 Rules That Actually Matter

Culture · 5 min read · By Nijimaru · June 2026
This article contains no affiliate links. About our disclosures →

Travel guides love listing 40 Japanese etiquette rules. Most of them are either obvious, rarely encountered, or not actually enforced. These 9 will genuinely affect your experience if you get them wrong.

Context first: Japanese people understand that tourists don't know all the rules. Breaking a minor one won't ruin your trip. Breaking one of these 9 repeatedly will make you visibly unwelcome.

1. No tipping — ever

Tipping is not part of Japanese culture. In restaurants, taxis, hotels, anywhere. Leaving cash on a table is confusing and sometimes offensive. The price on the menu is the price. Service is included by default. Don't tip.

2. Shoes off when entering homes (and some restaurants)

If there's a step up at the entrance (玄関, genkan) and a row of slippers, take your shoes off. This applies to private homes, many traditional restaurants, and some ryokan. Face your shoes toward the door when you remove them. Wear the slippers provided indoors — except in rooms with tatami mats, where you go barefoot or in socks.

Bathroom slippers: Many homes and traditional guesthouses have separate slippers in the bathroom. Swap into them when entering, swap back when leaving. The number of tourists who have walked through a Japanese home in toilet slippers is genuinely embarrassing.

3. Quiet on trains and buses

No phone calls. Conversations are kept low. Headphone audio should not be audible to others. This is not enforced by anyone official — it's just what everyone does, and violating it is very noticeable.

4. Walk on the left (mostly)

General guidance: keep left on sidewalks and escalators. On escalators, stand on the left, walk on the right — except in Osaka where it's reversed (stand right, walk left). Follow what the person in front of you is doing.

5. Don't eat while walking

Eating while walking through a neighborhood is considered messy and inconsiderate. Street food at stalls is fine — step aside, eat there, then move on. The exception is tourist areas (Asakusa, Harajuku's Takeshita Street) where vendors sell specifically for walking consumption.

6. Chopstick basics

Two specific things to avoid:

  • Sticking chopsticks upright in rice — resembles a funeral ritual
  • Passing food chopstick-to-chopstick — also a funeral ritual

Everything else — dropping them, using them wrong, using a fork instead — is fine and nobody will judge you for it.

7. Bow when receiving something

When someone hands you something (change, a business card, a ticket), a small nod or slight bow is appropriate. Not a deep bow — just an acknowledgment. Receiving a business card without looking at it is considered disrespectful in formal contexts; for tourist interactions it doesn't matter.

8. Smoking rules are strict

Smoking outdoors is prohibited in most city areas except designated smoking spots. Indoors: restaurants are increasingly non-smoking. Hotel smoking floors still exist. If you smoke, look for the designated 喫煙所 (kitsuen-jo) signs — usually a small enclosed area.

9. Sort your trash at konbini

Japan has a detailed trash sorting system. At convenience stores, there are multiple bins: burnable (燃えるゴミ), plastic (プラ), cans/bottles (缶・ビン). Do your best. Getting it slightly wrong is not a crisis, but ignoring the system entirely — or dumping all your trash in one bin — is noticeable.

The rule beneath the rules: Japanese public culture is built around not imposing on others. If you keep that principle in mind, you'll get most situations right instinctively.

The 9 rules

  • No tipping — ever
  • Shoes off at genkan
  • Quiet on transit
  • Walk left (mostly)
  • No eating while walking
  • Chopstick basics
  • Bow when receiving
  • Smoking zones only
  • Sort trash at konbini

Written by Nijimaru — a nurse living in Japan. I've seen tourists accidentally commit most of these. None of them got arrested. Most of the time, a Japanese person nearby quietly fixes the situation for you.
