What to Buy at a Japanese Convenience Store (And What to Skip)

Essentials · 5 min read · By Nijimaru · June 2026
This article contains no affiliate links. About our disclosures →

Every travel guide says "check out a Japanese convenience store." None of them tell you that you will spend 20 minutes staring at things you can't identify, buy the wrong thing, and miss the actually good stuff. This is the guide they should have written.

The big three

7-Eleven (セブン‐イレブン), Lawson (ローソン), and FamilyMart (ファミリーマート) are everywhere. There are slight quality differences but they're marginal. Go to whichever is nearest.

What to definitely buy

Onigiri (rice balls) — ¥130–180 / ~$0.85–1.20

The triangular wrapped rice balls. Every konbini makes excellent ones. Tuna mayo (ツナマヨ) is the safest pick for first-timers. The wrapper has a numbered pull-tab system — follow the numbers (1, 2, 3) to open it without losing the seaweed.

Egg salad sandwiches — ¥200–280 / ~$1.30–1.85

Sounds unambitious. It isn't. The egg salad sandwiches at Japanese convenience stores are genuinely excellent — creamy, lightly seasoned, on soft milk bread. 7-Eleven's version is the benchmark.

Hot foods counter — ¥150–350 / ~$1–2.30

The glass case near the register with rotating items. Karaage (fried chicken), nikuman (steamed pork buns), and croquettes are consistently good. Point at what you want. The staff will bag it.

Canned coffee — ¥120–160 / ~$0.80–1.05

Georgia MAX Coffee if you want sweet. BOSS if you want something closer to actual coffee. Both are in the warm drink section (the section with the orange/red labels on the refrigerator doors means warm).

Chu-hi (canned cocktails) — ¥180–250 / ~$1.20–1.65

Lemon-flavored sparkling alcohol. Cheap, good, and extremely Japanese. -196°C Strong Zero is the famous one. The "Strong" in the name is accurate — it's 9% alcohol.

The ATM — do not skip this

7-Bank ATMs (inside 7-Eleven) and the Japan Post ATMs (inside Lawson and FamilyMart) accept most foreign debit and credit cards. This is the most reliable way to get yen in Japan. Fee is around ¥110–220 / ~$0.75–1.45 per transaction.

Do this on day one: Find a 7-Eleven ATM and withdraw cash. Many places in Japan are cash-only, and running out on a Sunday evening is a genuinely bad situation.

What else is in there

  • Printing: Multifunction printers at the back of the store. Can print from USB or phone. In Japanese, but usually navigable by icon.
  • Package pickup/drop-off: Most konbini accept Yamato Transport and Japan Post packages. Handy for sending luggage ahead to your next hotel.
  • Charging cables: Often sold near the register. Overpriced but functional in an emergency.

What to skip

  • Pre-made pasta and noodle bowls: They exist, they're mediocre. Go to an actual noodle shop instead — there's one within 200 meters of wherever you are.
  • Energy drinks: Fine, but the same as everywhere. The interesting drinks are the local ones.
  • Expensive imported snacks: You didn't come to Japan to buy Pringles.

Paying

IC cards (Suica, Pasmo) work at all konbini. Credit cards work at most. Cash always works. Self-checkout lanes are common — the screen switches to English if you press the globe icon.

One last thing: Say ありがとうございます when you leave. Not required. Always noticed.

Must-buy list

  • Onigiri (tuna mayo)
  • Egg salad sandwich
  • Hot foods counter
  • Georgia coffee (warm)
