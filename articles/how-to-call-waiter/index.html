How to Call a Waiter in Japan (Without Being Rude)

Restaurants · 5 min read · By Nijimaru · June 2026
Heads up: This article contains no affiliate links. It's just useful information. About our disclosures →

You raise your hand. Nothing. You make eye contact and give a small wave. Still nothing. You clear your throat loudly. The waiter is six feet away, has definitely noticed you three times, and is deliberately not coming over.

This is not bad service. This is you doing it wrong. Here's the fix.

The one word you need

Say すみません (Sumimasen). Clearly. Once. At a normal speaking volume. A waiter within earshot will come.

すみません Excuse me / Hey Sumimasen Copy & hear it →

It's not a magic word. It's just the standard way to get someone's attention in Japan — street, shop, anywhere. In a restaurant it means "I need help." Every waiter knows it. None of them are offended by it.

Pronunciation tip: "Soo-mee-mah-sen." Four syllables, stress on the first. Don't trail off on the last syllable — that's how you make it sound hesitant.

Why waving doesn't work

In Japan, waiting staff are trained not to approach a table unless called. This is intentional — it's considered respectful to give diners privacy. A wave or eye contact doesn't count as being called. You have to actually say something.

Snapping fingers, clapping, or saying "hey" in English are all considered rude. Don't do any of those. Just say Sumimasen.

The hand signal (optional, but useful)

In a loud restaurant, you can combine the word with a slight raise of one hand — index finger extended, not frantic waving. Think of it as punctuation for the word, not a substitute for it. Hand only, no word = still no waiter.

Food ticket machines (食券機)

At many ramen shops, curry places, and some udon restaurants, you don't call a waiter at all. You order from a vending machine before you sit down.

Important: At restaurants with a food ticket machine, walking past it and sitting down is the wrong move. You'll get stared at and asked to go back to the machine.

Here's how to use a food ticket machine (食券機, shokuken-ki):

  1. Look for the machine near the entrance — it's usually obvious.
  2. Check for an English button (英語) in the corner. Not all machines have one, but more do every year.
  3. If no English: photos are on the buttons. Point at what looks good.
  4. Insert cash (most machines are cash only). The machine makes change.
  5. Take your ticket. Hand it to the staff when they come to your table, or leave it visible.

You'll know it's a food ticket restaurant before you even walk in — the machine is usually visible through the window or right inside the door.

Getting the check

In Japan, the check does not come automatically. You have to ask for it. Two ways:

お会計を
お願いします Check, please Okaikei wo onegaishimasu Copy & hear it →

Or use the X gesture: cross your two index fingers in an X shape and hold it up. This is universally understood in Japanese restaurants as "I'd like the check." No words required.

Table call buttons

Newer and higher-end restaurants often have a small call button at the table — usually labeled 呼出 (yobidashi) or with a bell icon. Press it once. A staff member will come within a minute or two. Don't press it repeatedly.

Tipping

Don't. Tipping is not part of Japanese restaurant culture and can make things awkward. Service is included in the price. Leave no cash on the table when you go.

Bottom line: Say Sumimasen. One word, normal volume, once. That's it. Everything else in this article is bonus knowledge.

Quick reference

  • すみません — call the waiter
  • 食券機 — order machine (no waiter)
  • お会計 / X gesture — get the check
